# Hick's Law

Hick's Law states that decision time increases with the number and complexity of available choices. It was established in 1952 by psychologists William Edmund Hick and Ray Hyman, who studied the relationship between the number of stimuli and a person's reaction time.

## Key Takeaways for UX Design

- **Minimize choices** when fast responses matter
- **Break complex tasks into smaller steps** to reduce cognitive load
- **Highlight recommended options** to avoid overwhelming users
- **Use progressive onboarding** to ease new users in gradually
- **Don't oversimplify** to the point of abstraction

## Real-World Examples

| Product | Application |
|---|---|
| Google's homepage | Strips away distractions to focus solely on the search input |
| Apple TV Remote | Offloads complexity to the TV interface, keeping the physical remote minimal |
| Slack's onboarding | Hides most features initially, introducing them gradually as users get comfortable |

## Core Tension

Hick's Law captures a fundamental challenge for designers: balancing *feature richness* with *clarity*. More options can mean more power, but also more friction. The goal is to surface the right choices at the right time rather than presenting everything at once.
